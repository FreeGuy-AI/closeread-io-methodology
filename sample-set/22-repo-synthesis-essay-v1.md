# 22 OSS Repositories, One Pattern: What Closeread Found That Buyers Already Knew

_Synthesis essay across the v0.4 sample set. Companion to [`INDEX.md`](../../../examples/INDEX.md) and the [findings-library](../findings-library/polyglot/)._

## TL;DR

The same nine patterns surfaced again and again across 22 production-grade OSS SaaS codebases written in seven different languages. None of the patterns are new and none of them are exotic. They are the things a buyer's counsel and a buyer's engineer were going to write into the diligence report anyway. The Closeread synthesis is that the seller can pre-write the report, and that doing so is worth more than fixing any single finding.

## Methodology note

This essay synthesizes 22 audits run by the Closeread pipeline between 2026-05-22 and 2026-05-24. Targets were chosen from the public OSS-SaaS-shape population, not from a paying customer roster: cal.com, papermark, ghost, plausible, twenty, khoj, formbricks, plane, listmonk, mealie, nocodb, penpot, tooljet, trilium-notes, umami, uptime-kuma, vikunja, activepieces, chatwoot, coolify, documenso, and Closeread itself. Each target was cloned at `--depth 1`, scanned by ten deterministic specialists (SCA, credential inventory, license, architecture, third-party API, security posture, test coverage, key-person, reliability, stack hireability), passed through an adversarial reviewer (OpenRouter rebut-pattern, Wynn et al. ICML MAS 2025), and rendered through the citation verifier before this synthesis read any of it. The 22 packets are referenced in the companion INDEX.md as Markdown, HTML, and PDF triples; this essay re-reads them, pattern-matches across them, and reports what survives. The reader should treat this essay as a methodology document, not a marketing artifact: every claim points to a specific packet and a specific finding ID that can be independently inspected.

## The nine patterns

### 1. The First-Hour Audit

Every buyer's engineer runs the same three commands in the first hour of repo access. The first is `npm audit` or its language equivalent (`bundle audit`, `pip-audit`, `mix deps.audit`, `cargo audit`). The second is a `git log` against `.env` patterns. The third is a grep for the framework's most common antipattern, which on a Next.js codebase is `process.env.* || ""`, on a Rails codebase is `Rails.application.credentials.dig(...)` without a fail-fast, on a Phoenix codebase is `System.get_env(...)` defaulting to nil in production config.

The engineer has done this enough times that the output reads to them like a chord, not a noise floor. Their next thirty minutes are spent reading what the chord told them. cal.com surfaces eight Next.js advisories at `apps/docs/package.json:11` that all collapse to a single version bump; the buyer's engineer would have gotten there in twenty minutes. Khoj surfaces ten HIGH SCA findings against `next@15.5.14` in `src/interface/web/package.json:65` that all collapse to the same upgrade. The packet shortens twenty minutes to one read. More importantly, the packet shortens the diligence-call airtime spent re-explaining what the engineer already knows; the seller's job moves from defending the codebase to walking the buyer through the remediation plan that is already in the data room.

The deal-impact pattern is consistent: an engineer who finds the same eight findings in twenty minutes that the seller pre-disclosed in two paragraphs walks into the diligence call as a collaborator, not as an adversary. The same engineer who has to discover those findings from scratch walks in with the suspicion that there is more buried elsewhere.

**Methodology pointer:** every Closeread packet leads with a "What the buyer's engineer will run" block and the exact shell commands. The pipeline does not invent its own taxonomy; it deterministically mirrors what acquirers already do, so the seller can pre-empt it line by line.

### 2. Dependency Drift Is the Universal Signal

Every audit in the sample surfaced out-of-date dependencies with known CVEs, regardless of language. cal.com had two CRITICAL Next.js advisories (FREE-0078 and FREE-0080, both CVE-2025-29927-class middleware authorization bypass) plus a `axios@1.15.0` cluster of three HIGH advisories at `apps/api/v2/package.json:65`. Plausible's tracker bundle shipped seven HIGH JavaScript CVEs (`flatted@3.3.3`, `picomatch@2.3.1`, `minimatch@3.1.2`, `picomatch@4.0.3`, `rollup@4.41.1`, `fast-uri@3.0.6`, `minimatch@5.1.6`) in a project whose Elixir backend is otherwise impeccable. khoj carried 124 raw advisories across PyPI and npm because the project pinned `next@15.5.14` once and `lxml@4.9.3` once and never came back. papermark shipped a Next 14.2.35 base with seven open advisories on a framework that runs every page load.

The pattern is not "this codebase is poorly maintained." The pattern is "shipping a feature is the thing the team prioritizes; updating dependencies is the thing the buyer asks about." The signal value of unpatched dependencies is rarely the exploitability of any individual CVE; it is the cadence question. Papermark's audit puts it precisely: "founder is not staying current, which compounds into the 'what other shortcuts did they take' question that drives the multiple down a half-turn."

The deal-impact of dependency drift is rarely existential. The cleanup is usually one engineer-day or less. cal.com's two CRITICAL findings clear with a fifteen-minute `next` bump in `apps/docs/package.json:11`; that same bump clears six of the eight HIGH findings in the same workstream. Papermark's seven Next.js advisories clear with a Next 14-to-15 migration that takes one to two days. The cost of leaving them is reputational: the buyer's engineer reads the `npm audit` output before they read the architecture document, and the count drives the qualitative judgment about everything else.

**Methodology pointer:** [`out-of-date-dependencies-with-known-cves.md`](../findings-library/polyglot/out-of-date-dependencies-with-known-cves.md). Closeread groups advisories by package and manifest, not by CVE count, so a buyer's "38 vulnerabilities" headline collapses to the "12 distinct packages, all bump-able" reality. The packet's cross-reference matrix lists each cleanup action by remediation cost: "Bump `next` in `apps/docs/package.json:11`: 15 min, resolves FREE-0078, FREE-0080, FREE-0069, FREE-0074, FREE-0076, FREE-0079, FREE-0086, FREE-0087."

### 3. The Monorepo Multiplier

NestJS workspaces, Next.js apps directories, and pnpm/turborepo monorepos all inflate the headline finding count without changing the underlying risk. twenty CRM is the canonical example: 22,519 files across 57 declared manifests, with `axios@1.14.0` in `twenty-website` and `axios@1.13.5` in `twenty-server`, producing 38 HIGH SCA findings against roughly 12 underlying vulnerable packages. cal.com's `apps/docs/package.json` carries Next.js 15.1.0 with eight separate advisories; one version bump in one file clears eight findings. plausible's `tracker/package-lock.json` carries `picomatch@2.3.1` nested under micromatch and `picomatch@4.0.3` at the top level, both vulnerable, both clearable with one `npm dedupe` plus one `npm update`. The lockfile shows the resolution path; the buyer's `npm audit` shows the count.

The math difference matters more than the math itself. A buyer's diligence engineer who reads "38 HIGH findings" without context priced in escrow against future-CVE liability; the same engineer who reads "12 distinct packages, all unblocked, all small, all owned by the framework upgrade cadence" prices in a hygiene-remediation milestone in the SPA. Both are real outcomes from the same underlying repository; the framing is the work.

The buyer's first read counts findings. The seller's job is to translate findings into workstreams. twenty CRM's packet does this explicitly: "the underlying vulnerable-package count is closer to 12, but a buyer's engineer running `npm audit` package-by-package will see all 38 and ask which ones reach production code paths. The remediation is a single PR that upgrades axios to the patched release in every workspace at once, then re-runs `nx affected` to ship the change. The work is small. The framing is the entire point."

**Methodology pointer:** [`monorepo-dependency-multiplier.md`](../findings-library/polyglot/monorepo-dependency-multiplier.md) (in development). The packet's cross-reference matrix groups findings by remediation action rather than by ID, so one row reads "Bump `next` in `apps/docs/package.json:11`" and lists the eight findings it clears with a single line-item effort estimate.

### 4. The Empty-String Fallback

The single highest-leverage finding in the sample set surfaced in papermark, not from gitleaks but from a grep for `process.env.* || ""`. The line at `lib/jackson.ts:18` reads:

```typescript
const encryptionKey = crypto
  .createHash("sha256")
  .update(process.env.NEXTAUTH_SECRET || "")
  .digest("base64")
  .substring(0, 32);
```

If `NEXTAUTH_SECRET` is ever unset, the SAML SSO encryption key derives from the SHA-256 of the empty string, which is a publicly-known constant (`e3b0c44298fc1c149afbf4c8996fb92427ae41e4649b934ca495991b7852b855`). Anyone who knows the antipattern exists can decrypt SAML connection data from any Papermark instance where the env var was misset. The code fix is four hours. The historical-exposure audit (searching every deployment environment's logs for any startup where the env var was missing, identifying every SAML connection touched in that window, and rotating those connections) is one week and a customer-comms event if any exposure is found. The asking-price defense is $50K to $200K.

The pattern repeated in three more files in the same repository: `OPENAI_API_KEY` at `lib/openai.ts:5`, `QSTASH_CURRENT_SIGNING_KEY` and `QSTASH_NEXT_SIGNING_KEY` at `lib/cron/index.ts:13-18`, all falling back to `""`. None of the four were caught by the off-the-shelf credential scanner. All four were caught by a manual regex pass that takes two seconds to run. The fallback-empty antipattern is structurally worse than fallback-default, because at least a default string is a fingerprint a scanner can detect; the empty-string fallback is invisible to scanners and identical across deployments.

This is the Closeread methodology's signature finding category. The deterministic scanners (gitleaks, trufflehog, OSV) miss this class entirely because no string was committed; the antipattern is a structural shortcut in the runtime configuration code, not a leaked secret. The cost-to-find is two seconds of grep. The cost-of-not-finding is asymmetric: catastrophic if exploited, invisible if not.

**Methodology pointer:** [`empty-string-secret-fallback.md`](../findings-library/polyglot/empty-string-secret-fallback.md). The grep is part of the credential specialist's checklist on every Node/TypeScript audit. The corresponding Elixir checklist greps for `System.get_env("X") || ""` and `System.get_env("X", "")`; the corresponding Python checklist greps for `os.environ.get("X", "")` with empty defaults on names that look like secrets; the corresponding Ruby checklist greps for `ENV["X"] || ""` and `ENV.fetch("X", "")`. The pattern is universal; the syntax varies by language.

### 5. The Test-Fixture Credential

Every credential scanner in 2026 flags PEM-shaped strings inside test files as HIGH severity. Every mature repository has them. cal.com committed nine SSH/PEM private keys to `apps/api/v2/src/modules/cal-unified-calendars/services/google-calendar.service.spec.ts`, plus two hardcoded bearer tokens in e2e specs (FREE-1129 and FREE-1130). Plausible committed `"Bearer redacted_access_token"` to `test/plausible/imported/google_analytics4_test.exs:208`, a placeholder string the team explicitly labeled with the word "redacted" but that the scanner pattern-matched on the `Bearer ...` prefix. twenty CRM committed three PEM private keys inside JWT-rotation integration tests; all three were correctly downgraded by the specialist pipeline but would have stopped a buyer's diligence call cold until the seller demonstrated fixture status.

The cost of fixture status is roughly zero if documented and meaningful if not. cal.com's nine keys could be rotated, regenerated at test runtime, or annotated; any of the three resolves the finding. The buyer's two paths split sharply: the cheap path is the seller demonstrating that every key is fixture data and the buyer accepting a representation in the SPA; the expensive path is the buyer requiring git history rewrite via `git filter-repo` or BFG before close, the seller absorbing one week of engineering time, and no one being happy.

twenty's `ENTERPRISE_VALIDITY_TOKEN` in `packages/twenty-server/.env.test` is the harder case. The token is a structurally valid RS256-signed JWT, ~660 characters long, claiming `sub=dev-subscription-id`, `status=valid`, with an exp deep in 2125. The token looks real because it is real, in the sense that something signed it with a real RS256 private key. The pipeline cannot tell whether the corresponding signing key is a one-off dev key (safe to commit) or the same key the production entitlement service uses (not safe to commit). The five-minute investigation is to confirm the signing-key origin before the data room opens, not after.

Ghost's case is the cleanest in the sample. Four MEDIUM credential findings (FREE-1051 through FREE-1054) in admin-UI React components all triage as variable-naming false positives when the source line is read in context: `const tinybirdTokenQuery = useTinybirdToken({enabled: hasStatsConfig});` triggers the entropy scorer on the word "Token" plus the surrounding React hook call, not on any committed string. The packet's job is to telegraph this disposition so the buyer's engineer reaches the same conclusion in seconds instead of minutes.

**Methodology pointer:** [`test-fixture-credentials-in-version-control.md`](../findings-library/polyglot/test-fixture-credentials-in-version-control.md). The Closeread credential specialist downgrades any finding in `test/`, `tests/`, `*_test.exs`, `*.spec.ts`, `*.test.ts`, or any path matching `*/fixtures/*` to LOW with a fixture-classification rationale rendered inline, then lists each one so the buyer's scanner sees the same surface with the explanation already attached. The MEDIUM and HIGH cases that survive the fixture-classification rule are the ones the seller actually needs to act on; everything else is pre-explained in the packet.

### 6. The Shallow-Clone Bus-Factor Mirage

Every audit run against a `--depth 1` clone reports the same finding: one author owns 100% of in-window commits. The finding is structurally inconclusive on a depth-one clone and would be misleading if rendered as a HIGH severity. Closeread fixed this in Day 8 as Bug 7; the bus-factor specialist now emits an INFO note instead of a misleading single-contributor concentration finding. The literal output ("100% of last 365 days commits from one author") is true of the clone window and uninformative about the codebase.

Ghost is the counter-example that proves the methodology caveat is load-bearing. The depth-one clone showed Hannah Wolfe authoring 100% of the single commit in the window, but Ghost has 200+ lifetime contributors across eight years of public history. The audit packet's job is to surface the shallow-clone caveat in writing so the buyer requests a deep-clone re-verification rather than treating the artifact as evidence. Ghost's packet says it plainly: "the audit headline on Ghost is not what the scan caught. It is what the scan cannot see from a 1-commit window into an 8-year project."

The legitimate bus-factor risk is harder and requires the deep clone the audit does not have. Papermark is genuinely a single-author codebase (Marc Seitz, 100% of last year's commits on the public repo, the figure verifiable by anyone who runs `git log --format='%aN' | sort | uniq -c` against the full history). The remediation is not a code change; it is a two-page operational handover document in the data room, naming the runbook for every production system Marc personally touches, a directory of where the tribal knowledge lives (Notion, Linear, Slack DMs, Marc's head), a proposed 6-month transition arc with specific milestones, and a list of the 3-5 sub-systems that would be hardest to inherit without Marc's input. That document is the difference between a buyer pricing in a 90-day transition and pricing in a 12-month founder-retention bonus.

The packet's contribution is the distinction between methodology artifact and real finding. Closeread reports the artifact honestly ("INFO, shallow clone bus-factor inconclusive, rerun against full clone for accurate data") and leaves the legitimate finding to the seller's data-room handover document. The buyer's job is to ask for the deep-clone re-verification; the methodology's job is to surface the question.

**Methodology pointer:** [`shallow-clone-bus-factor-inconclusive.md`](../findings-library/polyglot/shallow-clone-bus-factor-inconclusive.md) plus [`single-author-100-percent-commit-share.md`](../findings-library/polyglot/single-author-100-percent-commit-share.md). The two methodology entries fork the same observation into a methodology caveat (the first) and a real finding (the second), so the packet does not conflate them. Closeread's pre-Day-8 packets did conflate them; the bug was caught against uptime-kuma's audit and fixed before the v0.4 launch-polished render.

### 7. The License File That Does Not Match SPDX

Ghost ships under MIT. Every reader knows this. The `LICENSE` file at the repo root opens with `Copyright (c) 2013-2026 Ghost Foundation` before the standard MIT body, which causes automated SPDX matchers to fail on the first 1KB and route the file to a counsel queue instead of marking it MIT. The buyer's M&A counsel will read it in thirty seconds and recognize MIT; the buyer's automated portfolio-license scanner will not, and the buyer's counsel queue absorbs the false negative. The finding is real for an automated SPDX matcher and small in deal impact, but the disclosure belongs in the packet because automated buyer tooling will catch it too.

cal.com's `packages/types/ical.d.ts` declares `SPDX-License-Identifier: GPL-3.0-only` inside an otherwise-MIT repository, which a buyer's IP counsel reads as a one-question question: provenance. The answer determines whether the finding is cosmetic (copy-paste error in the header, contributor signed a CLA, file is clean-room) or structural (file was lifted from an upstream GPL project, derivative work exposes the rest of the codebase to GPL terms). One day of buyer's counsel time either way; one paragraph in the data room pre-empts the question.

twenty CRM ships AGPL-3.0 with an Enterprise commercial overlay. AGPL-3.0 is the network-strong copyleft. Many strategic acquirers in regulated industries (finance, healthcare, government) have a policy that excludes AGPL targets at the term-sheet stage, before any code review starts, because their internal IP policy treats source-availability as a leak vector for proprietary modifications. The Enterprise overlay softens this if the dual-license structure is disclosed and the CLA log is clean; without disclosure, the buyer's counsel spends the first week of diligence on AGPL interpretation. That week either gets billed back to the seller in deferred-close discount terms or shifts the closing date by 5-10 business days. At a typical $50K/month run rate, the slip is worth $7,500 to $15,000 in carry.

None of these are deal-killers. All of them are conversations that take one paragraph in the data room README to pre-empt and one to two weeks of buyer's-counsel time when not pre-empted.

**Methodology pointer:** [`copyleft-license-without-source-headers.md`](../findings-library/polyglot/copyleft-license-without-source-headers.md) and [`no-license-file-or-non-osi-license.md`](../findings-library/polyglot/no-license-file-or-non-osi-license.md). Four of 22 audits had license findings worth disclosure in the executive summary; the rest passed clean. Closeread's IP Ownership artifact opens with the SPDX-match status and the recommended counsel paragraph, in that order, so the buyer's counsel reads the disposition before they read the file.

### 8. The Vendor Concentration Map Buyers Always Build

Every buyer's engineer eventually draws a vendor topology on a whiteboard during the diligence call. cal.com has six critical-path vendors (Stripe, GCP, PayPal, AWS, Clerk, Cloudflare). Papermark has eight (S3, Stripe, OpenAI, Resend, Auth0, Trigger.dev, Upstash Redis, BoxyHQ SAML Jackson). Ghost has four (Stripe, AWS, Mailgun, GCP). The vendor count is not the signal; the lock-in profile is the signal. Papermark's Trigger.dev integration touches twenty files in `lib/trigger/` and would cost 6-8 weeks to swap to BullMQ + self-hosted Redis, or 4-6 weeks to Inngest. cal.com's Stripe and PayPal touch revenue directly, so the contractual review carries through to the SPA closing checklist.

The per-vendor risk decomposes into three categories: pricing exposure (the vendor can raise prices and the buyer is locked in), credential handover risk (the seller's account credentials must move to the buyer cleanly without disruption), and single-point-of-failure risk (an outage at the vendor breaks the product). Six critical-path vendors is on the high side for cal.com's size and the buyer will request per-vendor handoff documentation, recent pricing change history, and contractual protection clauses, especially around the payments providers because Stripe plus PayPal both touch revenue.

The packet's job is to pre-build the topology, name the per-vendor risks, and write the contingency paragraph the buyer would have asked for. Papermark's packet does this for Trigger.dev explicitly: "if Trigger.dev kicks Papermark off the platform, every async job stops within minutes. Document processing fails, exports fail, view tracking fails. Papermark continues to serve cached document views but breaks on every workflow that needs a queue. Migration time + cost estimate: 6-8 weeks to swap to BullMQ + self-hosted Redis, or 4-6 weeks to Inngest." The data room note is one to two pages. The diligence-call airtime saved is several hours.

The cross-cutting finding is that the audit always surfaces more vendors than the team thinks they have. The Closeread vendor specialist greps for the SDK signatures of 35 common SaaS vendors plus any `process.env.*_API_KEY` or `process.env.*_TOKEN` matches in source, then cross-references against `.env.example` to confirm the integration is wired in current code rather than legacy. The auto-pipeline missed Trigger.dev on its first pass against Papermark's repo because the SDK signatures had drifted across versions; the manual review caught it from the `package.json` scan and added it to the inventory.

**Methodology pointer:** [`third-party-vendor-inventory-with-critical-path-concentration.md`](../findings-library/polyglot/third-party-vendor-inventory-with-critical-path-concentration.md). The Closeread third-party specialist runs a per-vendor lock-in analysis that names replaceability cost in engineer-weeks, vendor stability (public-company stable, funded-startup stable, OSS-stable, at-risk), and contract type, so the buyer can build the vendor-handoff workstream into the SPA closing checklist directly from the packet table.

### 9. The Scanner Coverage Gap

The Closeread v0.4 architecture and security-posture specialists are Python-only. The architecture-walkthrough prompt and the bandit SAST scanner both require Python source to fire. cal.com is TypeScript-dominated, with 547,205 lines of TypeScript against zero lines of Python in the production tree; both artifacts returned 100/100 because the scanners did not run, not because the codebase is provably clean. Ghost is JavaScript-dominated; same result. Papermark is TypeScript; same result. The pipeline reports the coverage gap honestly in the packet's "Findings the packet did not surface" section, so the buyer treats the 100/100 as "scanner did not apply to this language" rather than "structurally clean."

The pattern shows up in multiple specialists. The reliability specialist returns 100/100 on Ghost because Sentry is detected and CI is wired, but the structural-only signal does not include achieved uptime or error-budget history; that data lives in the seller's Sentry account and a buyer who wants it should request a read-only 7-day Sentry seat under NDA. The test-coverage specialist returns "structural footprint only" because running the test suite is a Day 8+ scope and the current pipeline reports file counts and directory layout, not coverage percentage. The polyglot SAST gap is the largest of the three; v0.5 will wrap Semgrep for broader coverage.

This is the audit's most-honest disclosure. Every scanner has a coverage gap. The methodology's job is to name the gap explicitly, not to render a misleading score. v0.5 will wrap Semgrep for polyglot coverage; v0.4 ships with the gap documented per-artifact and per-section. Papermark's packet calls this the "Tier C limit" pattern: "I checked the dependency manifest and the import sites in `app/global-error.tsx` and `instrumentation.ts`, both look properly wired. I cannot verify the DSN is set, the project is active, or the error volume is sane. The fastest fix: grant the buyer's diligence engineer read-only Sentry access for 7 days under the NDA. Their report becomes 80% more confident with no work on your part."

The honest disclosure is also the seller's defense. A buyer who reads "scanner did not apply to this language" trusts the rest of the packet more than a buyer who reads "scanner passed everything." False confidence is worse for the deal than acknowledged gaps; the buyer always discovers the gap eventually, and the question is whether they discover it on the seller's terms or their own.

**Methodology pointer:** every artifact section in every packet opens with a "Method" block that names what was actually scanned, in what language, by which tool. When the tool does not apply, the section says so before listing any findings. The Closeread doctrine: the gap is more useful to the buyer than a false-confident score.

## What this means for buyers

The buyer's diligence engineer does not need a longer report; they need a faster one. A Closeread packet pre-runs the commands the engineer would have run, surfaces the findings the engineer would have surfaced, and writes the disposition the engineer would have written. The buyer's edge is not the scanner output; it is the time spent on the questions the scanner cannot answer. Buy the time back by giving the engineer a packet that pre-empts the first hour. The remediation cost on every audit in this sample sits between four hours (papermark's `NEXTAUTH_SECRET` fix) and fourteen engineer-days (cal.com's complete hygiene-remediation cluster). The cost of not knowing sits between a one-week SPA delay and a 5-10% price adjustment. Buyers who run their own second-opinion audit against the same open methodology can do so for under $25 in pipeline spend and 30 minutes of wall-clock time; the methodology, not the runtime, is the moat.

## What this means for sellers

The seller's job is not to ship a clean codebase to the buyer; the seller's job is to ship a clean narrative about the codebase. Every finding in this sample set is fixable. Every finding has a known remediation path. The lever is not the code change; it is the writeup. Papermark's `NEXTAUTH_SECRET` fix is four hours of engineering work and one paragraph in the data room README, and together those two artifacts close a finding that would otherwise hold a six-figure escrow tail. cal.com's eight Next.js advisories at `apps/docs/package.json:11` clear with a fifteen-minute version bump and a one-line note in the dependency-hygiene section of the data room. The 22 audits in this sample set all converge on the same seller move: write the buyer's report first, then fix the few items that move the report. The order of operations is load-bearing. Buyers who get the writeup before the diligence call hold a different conversation than buyers who have to ask for it.

## What this means for OSS maintainers

The OSS-as-SaaS founder pattern is structurally different from the venture-backed startup pattern, and the audit findings reflect it. Single-author concentration is the norm, not the exception. Test coverage is below median (papermark detected zero tests across 1,528 source files; the rest of the sample sat between 25% and 40% file ratio, well below the 60-70% production-SaaS median). Dependency-update cadence is what the founder did this month rather than what the team does on a sprint. None of these are wrong choices for the 0-to-product-market-fit phase; all of them become diligence questions the moment the project lists on an acquisition marketplace. The maintainers who pre-write the operational handover document, ship the version-bump PR, label the test fixtures, and write the vendor contingency paragraph are the ones who close their deals at the asking price. The maintainers who do not are the ones who close at the buyer's counter, six weeks later, with a retention bonus structured around their continued involvement.

The methodology is open. The findings library is published. Every rule and every severity heuristic is verifiable. The maintainers who want to self-audit can clone https://github.com/FreeGuy-AI/closeread and run the same pipeline against their own repository before any buyer ever sees it. The audit costs them under $2 in OpenRouter spend at typical codebase scale. That is the point: the methodology, not the runtime, is the source of trust, and the trust is forkable.
